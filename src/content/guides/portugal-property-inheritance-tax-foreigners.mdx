# Portugal Property Inheritance Tax for Foreigners

Quick Answer: Portugal does not charge a classic inheritance tax for close family inheriting Portuguese property. Spouses, children, grandchildren, parents, and grandparents are exempt from the 10% stamp duty that applies to many other heirs. The larger risk for foreign owners is not only tax. It is succession law, forced heirship, cross-border documents, IMI, AIMI, and whether heirs can sell or hold the property without conflict.

Inheritance planning is easy to ignore when you are negotiating a villa in Cascais or an apartment in Lisbon. The purchase file feels urgent: [NIF](/guides/nif-portugal-property-purchase/), CPCV, [stamp duty](/guides/stamp-duty-portugal-property/), IMT, bank transfer, escritura. Succession feels distant until a family member dies and the heirs discover that Portuguese property cannot be transferred with a foreign death certificate alone.

This guide explains how Portuguese property inheritance works for foreign owners and foreign heirs in 2026. It covers stamp duty on inheritance, succession rules, EU and non-EU document differences, forced heirship, annual holding taxes, AIMI exposure, and the planning steps that prevent a good asset from becoming a family dispute.

## Does Portugal Have Inheritance Tax on Property?

Portugal has no standalone inheritance tax like the UK inheritance tax or US estate tax. Instead, inherited Portuguese assets can be subject to stamp duty, called Imposto do Selo, under specific rules. The key rate is 10%, but close family members are exempt.

The tax base is normally the property's Valor Patrimonial Tributário, or VPT. VPT is the official tax value registered with Autoridade Tributária. It can be below market value, especially for older properties, but heirs should not assume that a low historic VPT will last forever. Renovation, revaluation, or updated municipal coefficients can change the number used for future [IMI property tax](/guides/imi-property-tax-portugal/) and [AIMI wealth tax](/guides/aimi-wealth-tax-portugal-property/).

| Heir category | 10% stamp duty on inheritance? | Practical note |
|---|---:|---|
| Spouse | No | Exempt close family heir |
| Child, grandchild, great-grandchild | No | Exempt descendant line |
| Parent, grandparent | No | Exempt ascendant line |
| Sibling | Yes | Not in exempt close-family group |
| Unmarried partner | Usually yes | Cohabitation does not equal spouse automatically |
| Niece, nephew, cousin | Yes | 10% can apply on VPT |
| Friend or unrelated beneficiary | Yes | 10% can apply on VPT |
| Company or foundation beneficiary | Yes | Needs specialist review |

This distinction is the main reason a will and ownership plan matter. A British buyer who leaves an Algarve apartment to a spouse may create no Portuguese stamp duty. The same buyer leaving the asset to a long-term unmarried partner can create a 10% charge, even if the partner was the emotional and financial household spouse in practice.

For buyers still at acquisition stage, read this guide alongside [buy property in Portugal as a foreigner](/guides/buy-property-portugal-foreigner/) and the [stamp duty Portugal property guide](/guides/stamp-duty-portugal-property/). Stamp duty at purchase and stamp duty at inheritance are different events, but both use fixed legal categories that should be modelled before signatures.

## How Succession Rules Work in Portugal

Portuguese succession law gives protected shares to forced heirs. The protected group normally includes a surviving spouse, descendants, and in some cases ascendants. A property owner cannot always leave the entire Portuguese estate to whoever they want if Portuguese succession law applies.

For a foreign owner, the first question is which country's succession law governs the estate. Since the EU Succession Regulation, many cross-border estates in Europe are governed by the law of the deceased person's habitual residence, unless the person made a valid choice of the law of their nationality in a will. This choice is often called a professio juris.

Portugal participates in the EU Succession Regulation. The UK, Ireland, and Denmark did not opt in, but the regulation can still matter for Portuguese assets because Portuguese authorities apply it when handling property in Portugal. Non-EU nationals can still be affected when Portuguese courts or notaries determine the law applicable to Portuguese real estate.

| Succession issue | Portuguese default | Planning lever |
|---|---|---|
| Protected heirs | Spouse, descendants, sometimes ascendants | Review forced heirship before gift or will |
| Law governing estate | Often habitual residence | Nationality-law election in will where available |
| Portuguese property transfer | Requires local tax and registry steps | Portuguese lawyer and notary file |
| Foreign documents | Must be recognised in Portugal | Apostille, translation, certified copies |
| Multiple wills | Can conflict across countries | Coordinate Portuguese and home-country wills |

A common planning error is assuming a home-country will alone controls everything. It may express intent, but if it was drafted without Portuguese property language, local tax ID references, or succession-law election, heirs may spend months proving meaning to a notary. Another error is adding a Portuguese will that accidentally revokes the foreign will. Use coordinated documents, not template documents from two countries that ignore each other.

## EU Heirs vs Non-EU Heirs

EU and non-EU heirs generally face the same Portuguese stamp duty rules. A French child and a Canadian child are both exempt as descendants. A German sibling and a Brazilian sibling can both fall outside the exemption and face 10% stamp duty. The tax law does not make the EU passport the deciding factor.

The practical difference is administration. EU civil documents often move more easily through multilingual certificates and familiar notary channels. Non-EU heirs usually need apostilles, certified translations into Portuguese, and more detailed identity checks. If a bank account, sale proceeds, or mortgage discharge is involved, compliance reviews can take longer for heirs resident outside the EU.

| Item | EU heir | Non-EU heir |
|---|---|---|
| Stamp duty exemption for child or spouse | Same exemption | Same exemption |
| Death certificate recognition | Often simpler | Apostille often required |
| Translation needs | Sometimes reduced | Usually certified translation |
| NIF setup | Required if heir becomes owner | Required if heir becomes owner |
| Bank compliance | Standard KYC | Enhanced KYC more common |
| Expected simple transfer timeline | 2-4 months | 4-8 months |

The NIF point is often missed. If heirs inherit and keep the property, they need Portuguese tax identification and a fiscal address or representative structure where required. That allows IMI notices, condominium payments, insurance, and future sale paperwork to flow correctly.

Non-EU families should prepare a source-of-funds and source-of-wealth trail even though inheritance itself is not a purchase. If the inherited property is sold and proceeds move to a bank in the US, UAE, Brazil, or Singapore, the receiving bank may ask for the Portuguese deed, inheritance file, tax clearance, and proof that the heir had legal title.

## Stamp Duty on Inheritance: Worked Examples

Stamp duty on inheritance is usually 10% for non-exempt heirs. The calculation normally uses VPT rather than market price. That can reduce the immediate charge when VPT is below market value, but it can also mislead heirs about the property's real holding cost because IMI and AIMI also depend on VPT.

| Scenario | Market value | VPT | Heir | Stamp duty result |
|---|---:|---:|---|---:|
| Lisbon apartment to spouse | €520,000 | €390,000 | Spouse | €0 |
| Algarve villa to two children | €900,000 | €640,000 | Descendants | €0 |
| Porto flat to brother | €320,000 | €240,000 | Sibling | €24,000 |
| Cascais home to unmarried partner | €750,000 | €580,000 | Partner | €58,000 |
| Silver Coast house to friend | €280,000 | €210,000 | Unrelated | €21,000 |

**Example 1: spouse inherits an Algarve apartment**

A US non-resident owns a €480,000 Algarve apartment with VPT of €360,000. The surviving spouse inherits under a coordinated will. Portuguese stamp duty on inheritance is €0 because spouses are exempt. The spouse still needs to complete the tax and land registry transfer, update IMI billing, and decide whether to hold or sell.

**Example 2: unmarried partner inherits a Cascais townhouse**

A Canadian owner leaves a Cascais townhouse to a long-term partner. They were not married and had no Portuguese civil partnership recognised as a spouse equivalent for this purpose. VPT is €620,000. Potential stamp duty is €62,000. The couple may have lived exactly like a family, but the tax category can still be harsh.

**Example 3: sibling inherits a Porto rental**

A French resident leaves a Porto rental apartment to his sister. VPT is €230,000. The sister faces possible stamp duty of €23,000 and also inherits landlord obligations, tenant contract terms, IMI, insurance, and Portuguese rental reporting if she keeps the unit.

These examples do not replace legal advice because facts matter. Marriage regime, habitual residence, the will's governing-law clause, and whether there are forced heirs can change the succession result. They do show why heirs should not wait until after death to learn the Portuguese category rules.

## Annual Taxes After Inheritance: IMI and AIMI

Inheritance tax is only one moment. Once heirs become owners, Portuguese annual property taxes continue. IMI is the municipal property tax charged on VPT. AIMI is the additional property wealth tax that can apply above certain VPT thresholds.

The first cash-flow problem for heirs is often not the inheritance stamp duty. It is the period between death and final transfer. The property still has condominium fees, utilities, insurance, security, repairs, municipal tax, and sometimes mortgage or tenant issues. If heirs disagree, no one pays on time. Penalties and forced-sale pressure can build quickly.

| Holding tax | Trigger | Why heirs should care |
|---|---|---|
| IMI | Annual municipal property tax on VPT | Continues after death and after transfer |
| AIMI | Additional tax on high VPT holdings | Can apply if heir's share exceeds threshold |
| Condominium fees | Apartment or resort-managed property | Unpaid fees can block smooth sale |
| Rental income tax | If inherited property is rented | Heir may need Portuguese filing |
| Capital gains tax | Later sale by heir | Basis usually starts from inherited value |

AIMI planning is especially important for families inheriting Lisbon, Cascais, Estoril, Comporta, or Algarve assets. A single child inheriting 100% of a high-VPT villa may cross the AIMI threshold. Two children each inheriting 50% may remain below the threshold, depending on existing Portuguese assets. That tax saving must be weighed against future decision friction: two heirs must agree on sale price, renovation budget, rental strategy, and timing.

For the detailed annual tax mechanics, use the [AIMI wealth tax guide](/guides/aimi-wealth-tax-portugal-property/) and [IMI property tax guide](/guides/imi-property-tax-portugal/). For exit modelling after heirs sell, cross-read [Portugal capital gains tax on property](/guides/portugal-capital-gains-tax-property/).

## Estate Planning Structures for Foreign Owners

Good estate planning starts before the CPCV, not after the deed. The ownership structure chosen at purchase affects tax, control, bank financing, succession, and future sale. There is no universal best structure for foreign buyers.

Individual ownership is simplest. It is easier for bank mortgages, easier for notaries, and easier for heirs to understand. Joint ownership between spouses or family members can spread AIMI exposure and keep control inside the family. Company ownership can help in some portfolio cases but often adds accounting, corporate tax, UBO disclosure, bank complexity, and harder succession mechanics.

| Structure | Succession advantage | Tax and process risk |
|---|---|---|
| Sole individual owner | Simple title and sale process | One estate controls entire asset |
| Spouses joint ownership | Surviving spouse already owns share | Forced heirship still affects deceased share |
| Parent plus adult child | May reduce probate friction later | Gift and control issues need advice |
| Sibling co-ownership | Spreads AIMI exposure | High dispute risk on sale and repairs |
| Portuguese company | Useful for some portfolios | Accounting, IRC, UBO and bank friction |
| Foreign company | Sometimes used by family offices | Higher compliance and possible tax drag |

For most lifestyle buyers, the best plan is boring: buy personally or jointly, keep clean source-of-funds records, draft coordinated wills, and leave heirs a document pack. For investors with 3 or more Portuguese assets, company or family holding structures deserve review, but they should be judged against total tax and admin cost, not only inheritance optics.

## What Foreigners Should Put in a Portugal Property Will

A will for Portuguese property should be specific enough for a Portuguese lawyer and notary to use. It should identify the owner, the property, the chosen succession law where available, the intended heirs, and the local professional who can help the family start the file.

The will should not be written in isolation from the home-country estate plan. If you have assets in the UK, US, Brazil, France, South Africa, or the UAE, your advisers must coordinate. A Portuguese will can be limited to Portuguese assets, but it must avoid language that revokes earlier wills globally unless that is intentional.

Practical will checklist:

1. Identify the Portuguese property by address, land registry number, and matrix article if available.
2. State whether the will covers only Portuguese assets or the worldwide estate.
3. Include a clear succession-law election if available and recommended.
4. Name heirs with passport details and relationship.
5. Address forced heirship and reserved shares with legal advice.
6. Nominate a lawyer or executor contact who knows the property file.
7. Keep copies of purchase deed, caderneta predial, IMI receipts, insurance, mortgage documents, and condominium contacts.
8. Review after marriage, divorce, birth of children, change of tax residence, or second Portuguese purchase.

## Buyer Scenarios: Who Needs Planning Most?

Inheritance planning is not equally urgent for every foreign buyer. A married couple buying a €280,000 Porto apartment with two adult children has a different risk profile from an unmarried couple buying a €1.2M Cascais villa or a family office buying three Algarve rentals.

| Buyer profile | Main risk | Planning priority |
|---|---|---|
| Married EU couple, one apartment | Document clarity | Coordinated will and NIF pack |
| Unmarried partners | 10% stamp duty and forced heir conflict | Marriage, ownership split or will review |
| Blended family with children from prior marriage | Forced heirship dispute | Reserved-share modelling before purchase |
| Non-EU investor with rental property | Compliance and transfer delays | Apostilled document plan and local lawyer |
| High-value Cascais or Algarve owner | AIMI after transfer | Ownership split and VPT review |
| Elderly buyer using mortgage | Insurance, debt and estate liquidity | Loan discharge and liquidity plan |

The highest-risk group is unmarried partners with expensive property. They often assume the survivor will inherit naturally. Portuguese tax and succession categories can say otherwise. If one partner has children from a previous relationship, forced heirship can create a shared ownership situation between the survivor and children who may not want to co-own.

## Pros and Cons of Common Planning Moves

Estate planning can reduce tax and delay, but every move has tradeoffs. Do not transfer title, add children, or create companies only because it sounds tax efficient. The structure must still work if someone needs to sell, refinance, divorce, or move tax residence.

| Planning move | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|
| Coordinated Portuguese will | Clear local process, lower notary friction | Needs review with home-country will |
| Joint ownership with spouse | Survivor already owns part of asset | Deceased share still enters succession |
| Add adult child at purchase | May reduce later transfer friction | Gives child legal control now |
| Keep large cash reserve | Pays IMI, fees and legal costs during estate | Idle liquidity has opportunity cost |
| Company ownership | Can fit portfolio planning | Higher admin and tax complexity |
| Sell before death and gift cash | Simplifies property transfer | CGT and family-law issues can arise |

The cleanest plan is often not the one with the lowest theoretical tax. It is the one your heirs can execute without litigation, missing documents, or a forced sale at a bad price.

## Red Flags Before You Sign a CPCV

Pause the purchase and get specialist advice if any of these apply:

- You are buying with an unmarried partner and want the survivor to inherit.
- You have children from a previous marriage and want unequal shares.
- You intend to leave Portuguese property to a sibling, niece, nephew, friend, or charity.
- Your will was drafted before buying Portuguese property.
- Your home-country will says it revokes all previous wills and you also made a Portuguese will.
- You are buying above the AIMI threshold and have not checked VPT.
- You expect heirs outside the EU to manage the estate without a Portuguese lawyer.
- The property has a mortgage and life insurance terms are unclear.

Mortgage-linked life insurance can be useful, but it is not an estate plan. It may repay the bank while leaving title, forced heirship, and taxes unresolved. ## Portuguese Estate Field Note: Family Planning Before Tax Planning

Portuguese Estate is the MORE Group editorial and advisory desk for portuguese-estate.com. In cross-border property files, we see one pattern repeatedly: families ask about inheritance tax, but the real problem is control. Who can sign? Who has the NIF? Who pays IMI while the estate is open? Who decides whether to sell?

Our practical sequence for foreign buyers is:

1. Confirm who should inherit the property and whether that person is a spouse, descendant, ascendant, or taxable heir.
2. Check forced heirship before promising the asset to one person.
3. Model stamp duty, IMI, AIMI, condominium fees, and future [capital gains tax](/guides/portugal-capital-gains-tax-property/).
4. Choose ownership before CPCV, not after escritura.
5. Leave heirs a document folder with deed, caderneta predial, insurance, IMI receipts, mortgage details, lawyer contact, and login instructions.
